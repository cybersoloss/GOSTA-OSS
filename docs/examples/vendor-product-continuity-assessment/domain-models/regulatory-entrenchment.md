# Domain Model: Regulatory Entrenchment

**Domain Code:** REG-1
**Source:** Domain expertise — synthesized from publicly available regulatory frameworks including DORA (Digital Operational Resilience Act), NIS2 Directive, GDPR, EBA/EIOPA/ESMA ICT outsourcing guidelines, and general regulatory impact assessment methodology for third-party risk management
**Enhancement Sources:** None
**Application Context:** Evaluating how regulatory requirements both protect vendor viability (by creating demand) and amplify organizational exposure (by increasing exit costs) — a dual-nature assessment that must be scored in both directions
**Created:** 2026-04-20
**Purpose:** Provides evaluation criteria for assessing the regulatory dimension of vendor-product dependency, covering demand creation that supports viability AND compliance embedding that amplifies switching costs. This model has a unique dual nature: regulatory factors simultaneously support viability (positive) and increase exposure (negative), and both directions must be assessed independently

---

## 1. Core Concepts

### Regulatory Demand Creation

Regulations that mandate the type of capability a vendor provides create structural demand that supports vendor viability. When a regulation requires organizations to implement specific technical controls (e.g., endpoint detection, log retention, access management, encryption), vendors providing those capabilities benefit from non-discretionary purchasing. This demand is resilient to economic downturns because compliance spending is mandatory, not optional.

Assess by mapping: Which current regulations require the category of capability this vendor provides? How many regulated organizations fall within the vendor's addressable market? Is the regulatory trend expanding or contracting these requirements?

**Boundary:** Regulatory demand creation covers demand generated by compliance obligations. It does NOT cover demand from voluntary security frameworks (SOC 2, ISO 27001 adoption by choice), which creates market pressure but not regulatory mandate.

**Common misapplication:** Assuming regulatory demand protects a specific vendor rather than the capability category. Regulations require endpoint detection — they do not require a specific vendor's endpoint detection product. Regulatory demand supports category viability, which benefits individual vendors only to the degree that switching costs exist.

### Compliance Workflow Embedding

The depth to which a vendor-product is integrated into an organization's compliance processes: audit evidence generation, regulatory reporting, compliance dashboards, control attestation workflows, and regulator communication channels. A product that generates evidence used in regulatory submissions has higher compliance stickiness than one that provides a capability without participating in compliance documentation.

Score by examining: Does this product generate audit evidence cited in regulatory filings? Is it referenced in the organization's control framework documentation? Would replacing it require updating regulatory submissions or re-performing audits?

**Boundary:** Covers integration into compliance processes specifically. General operational integration is covered by STICK-1 (Integration Depth).

**Common misapplication:** Conflating product usage with compliance embedding. A product can be heavily used operationally but absent from compliance workflows, or lightly used operationally but critical to regulatory reporting. The compliance embedding score reflects regulatory process integration, not usage volume.

### Exit Provision Requirements

Multiple regulatory frameworks now mandate that organizations maintain exit strategies for critical third-party services. DORA Article 28 requires financial entities to develop exit plans for critical ICT third-party providers. EBA Guidelines on outsourcing require documented exit strategies including data portability provisions. These requirements mean that organizations must maintain actionable exit plans — which paradoxically increases awareness of switching costs and may accelerate migration decisions when viability concerns emerge.

Assess by mapping: Which regulations applicable to the organization require exit planning for this vendor category? Does the organization's current exit plan meet regulatory requirements? Has the exit plan been tested or validated?

**Boundary:** Covers regulatory mandates for exit planning. Does NOT cover the quality or feasibility of the exit plan itself, which is a STICK-1 concern.

**Common misapplication:** Treating the existence of a regulatory-mandated exit plan as evidence that exit is feasible. The plan's existence satisfies the regulatory requirement; its operational feasibility is a separate assessment.

### Regulatory Switching Costs

Beyond technical migration costs (covered by STICK-1), regulated organizations face additional switching costs that are purely regulatory in nature: re-certification of the new vendor against applicable frameworks, re-auditing controls that change as a result of the vendor switch, updating regulatory filings and control documentation, and potential supervisory notification requirements. For organizations subject to DORA, NIS2, or equivalent frameworks, vendor changes affecting critical services may require regulatory notification and approval.

Estimate by cataloging: Which regulatory certifications reference this vendor? Which audit reports include this vendor's controls? Which regulatory submissions would need updating? What supervisory notification or approval processes apply?

**Boundary:** Covers compliance-specific switching costs that add to technical migration costs. Does NOT duplicate technical migration cost estimation from STICK-1.

**Common misapplication:** Omitting re-certification timelines. A new vendor may need 6-12 months to achieve the certifications the departing vendor already holds, creating a compliance gap during transition that requires interim risk acceptance or compensating controls.

### Concentration Risk Under Regulation

Multiple regulatory frameworks penalize or flag single-vendor concentration. DORA's oversight framework specifically addresses concentration risk in critical ICT service providers. EBA guidelines require concentration risk assessment for material outsourcing. NIS2 implicitly addresses supply chain concentration. Organizations using a single vendor for multiple critical functions may face regulatory pressure to diversify — which can be either a viability risk (forced migration away from the vendor) or a viability support (regulatory barriers to competitors entering create defensible positions).

Assess by mapping: Does the organization use this vendor for multiple functions that regulators might classify as concentrated? Have regulators issued guidance on concentration in this vendor's category? Is the vendor designated or likely to be designated as a critical third-party provider under applicable oversight frameworks?

**Boundary:** Covers regulatory treatment of vendor concentration. Does NOT cover business concentration risk (the operational impact of depending on one vendor), which is a general risk management concern.

**Common misapplication:** Treating concentration risk as automatically negative for the vendor. Regulatory concentration scrutiny can create barriers to entry for competitors (new entrants face the same oversight burden) and increase switching costs (regulatory approval required to change).

### Regulatory Assessment Obligations

Ongoing due diligence mandates require organizations to periodically reassess third-party vendors. DORA requires continuous monitoring of critical ICT providers. EBA guidelines mandate periodic review of outsourcing arrangements. GDPR Article 28 requires processors to demonstrate ongoing compliance. These obligations create recurring assessment cycles that generate evidence about vendor viability — which means regulatory requirements can serve as an early warning system for viability concerns if the assessment process is substantive rather than perfunctory.

Assess by mapping: What periodic assessment obligations apply to this vendor relationship? Are current assessments substantive (testing controls, reviewing financials, assessing continuity plans) or checkbox exercises?

**Boundary:** Covers regulatory mandates for ongoing vendor assessment. Does NOT cover voluntary vendor management best practices.

**Common misapplication:** Treating annual questionnaire completion as satisfying regulatory assessment obligations. Most regulatory frameworks require risk-proportionate assessment depth — a one-size-fits-all questionnaire for a critical vendor may not satisfy the regulatory standard.

### Forward-Looking Regulatory Exposure

Upcoming regulations that will change the assessment landscape. The EU AI Act introduces requirements for AI system providers that will affect vendors embedding AI capabilities. Evolving data sovereignty requirements may affect cloud-hosted vendors. Proposed regulations on algorithmic transparency may require vendors to expose decision logic. The assessment must consider not only current regulatory requirements but regulations in legislative pipeline (proposed, adopted-but-not-yet-effective, and effective-but-enforcement-pending).

Assess by scanning: What proposed or adopted regulations will affect this vendor's category within the next 24 months? Will these regulations increase or decrease regulatory demand? Will they increase or decrease switching costs?

**Boundary:** Covers regulations in the legislative pipeline that have progressed beyond discussion to formal proposal or adoption. Does NOT cover speculative future regulation based on political trends.

**Common misapplication:** Weighting proposed regulations equally with enacted ones. Apply a probability discount: enacted-but-not-yet-effective regulations are near-certain (0.9 weight), formally proposed regulations carry moderate probability (0.5-0.7), and publicly discussed concepts carry low probability (0.1-0.3).

### Customer Tier Distinction

Regulatory entrenchment varies significantly by customer segment for the same vendor-product. Enterprise customers in regulated industries (financial services, healthcare, critical infrastructure) typically embed vendor-products deeply into compliance workflows, with 15-30 regulatory touchpoints per critical vendor. SMB customers using the same product for operational purposes may have 2-5 regulatory touchpoints. This creates a 2-3 point difference in regulatory entrenchment scores for the same product assessed from different customer perspectives.

When assessing regulatory entrenchment, explicitly identify the customer tier perspective: enterprise-regulated, enterprise-unregulated, or SMB. Apply scoring adjustments accordingly.

**Boundary:** Covers variation in regulatory entrenchment by customer type. Does NOT cover customer satisfaction or product quality variation by tier.

**Common misapplication:** Applying enterprise-regulated entrenchment scores to the general customer base. If 20% of customers are enterprise-regulated and 80% are SMB, the regulatory demand creation score should reflect the weighted exposure, not the maximum.

## 2. Concept Relationships

**Prerequisites:** Regulatory Demand Creation must be assessed before Concentration Risk Under Regulation — you need to know which regulations create demand before assessing whether that demand creates concentration concerns. Compliance Workflow Embedding must be mapped before Regulatory Switching Costs can be estimated — switching costs depend on what compliance workflows must be rebuilt.

**Tensions:** Regulatory Demand Creation (a viability support) creates tension with Exit Provision Requirements (which facilitate departure). Regulations simultaneously create the demand that sustains the vendor and mandate the exit planning that enables customers to leave. Concentration Risk Under Regulation creates tension with Regulatory Demand Creation — concentration scrutiny may force diversification away from a vendor whose demand is regulatory-created. The resolution depends on whether the organization is above or below regulatory concentration thresholds.

**Amplifiers:** Forward-Looking Regulatory Exposure amplifies all other concepts — upcoming regulations can increase demand creation, deepen compliance embedding, raise switching costs, and intensify concentration scrutiny simultaneously. Customer Tier Distinction amplifies the accuracy of all other assessments by calibrating scores to the appropriate customer segment.

## 3. Quality Principles

- **QP-1:** Dual-direction scoring — Every regulatory factor must be scored in both directions: viability support (how much does this regulatory factor protect the vendor's market position?) and exposure amplification (how much does this regulatory factor increase organizational switching costs?). A single unidirectional score is incomplete. Evaluate by checking that every regulatory concept produces two scores.
- **QP-2:** Regulation-specific citation — Assessments must cite specific regulatory provisions (article numbers, guideline sections) rather than general regulatory categories. "DORA Article 28(8) exit strategy requirements" is acceptable; "regulations require exit planning" is not. Evaluate by checking citation specificity.
- **QP-3:** Enacted vs. proposed distinction — Regulatory factors must be classified by status (enacted and effective, enacted but not yet effective, formally proposed, under discussion) with explicit probability weighting for non-effective provisions. Evaluate by checking whether the assessment distinguishes regulatory status levels.
- **QP-4:** Customer tier calibration — Regulatory entrenchment scores must specify the customer tier perspective from which they are assessed. An unqualified score is assumed to be the assessor's organization's perspective, but cross-references to other customer tiers should be noted when they materially differ. Evaluate by checking for explicit tier identification.
- **QP-5:** Regulatory-technical cost separation — Regulatory switching costs (re-certification, re-audit, regulatory notification) must be estimated separately from technical switching costs (data migration, integration rebuild) and then combined. Mixing them in a single estimate prevents accurate sensitivity analysis. Evaluate by checking for separated cost categories.
- **QP-6:** Viability-exposure independence — The viability-support score and the exposure-amplification score must be produced by independent analytical processes. A high viability-support score must not suppress the exposure-amplification score, and vice versa. Evaluate by checking whether viability and exposure arguments reference each other's conclusions.
- **QP-7:** Concentration threshold awareness — When a vendor is likely to be designated as a critical third-party provider under applicable oversight frameworks, the assessment must explicitly flag the concentration implications and potential regulatory-forced diversification requirements. Evaluate by checking whether critical-provider designation risk is addressed.

## 4. Anti-Patterns

- **AP-1:** Viability-exposure conflation — Treating regulatory demand creation (a viability support) and compliance embedding (an exposure amplifier) as a single factor, producing a score that obscures the dual nature. This leads to assessments where strong regulatory demand is used to dismiss switching cost concerns, or where high switching costs are used to argue vendor viability. Detect by checking whether viability and exposure are scored on separate scales. Address by requiring dual-direction scoring per QP-1.
- **AP-2:** Regulatory checkbox assessment — Performing regulatory assessment obligations (QP-6) through standardized questionnaires that do not vary by vendor criticality, producing uniform outputs that satisfy the letter of regulatory requirements while providing no analytical value. Detect by comparing assessment depth across vendors of different criticality levels — if they are identical, the process is checkbox-driven. Address by implementing risk-proportionate assessment depth tied to vendor criticality classification.
- **AP-3:** Static regulatory landscape assumption — Assessing regulatory entrenchment based solely on current regulations without considering the legislative pipeline. Given that major regulatory frameworks (DORA, NIS2, AI Act) have 2-4 year implementation timelines, a current-state-only assessment will miss regulatory changes that are certain to affect the assessment within its validity period. Detect by checking whether the assessment references any proposed or upcoming regulations. Address by requiring Forward-Looking Regulatory Exposure analysis for any assessment with a validity period exceeding 12 months.
